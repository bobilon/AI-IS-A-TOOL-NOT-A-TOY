# AI-IS-A-TOOL-NOT-A-TOY
AI IS A TOOL, NOT A TOY
And the Public Is About to Fritter Away the Most Significant Dividend in the History of Technology
A position paper. First draft, public record, 2026.
With appendices: The E-Model; The Regional Model; The Policy Slate.
The free lunch had a price. It always does. Two decades of no-cost, frictionless, algorithmically optimized technology did not arrive as generosity. It arrived as a business model — the most profitable business model in human history — and it worked by trading something the recipients did not know they were spending: their capacity for deliberate thought. You cannot run an operant conditioning system on a population for twenty years and then hand that same population a genuinely powerful tool and expect them to know what to do with it. The toy reflex is now the default. The scroll is the instinct. And the people who engineered that reflex are now positioning themselves to capture the value of AI before the public figures out that what they are holding is infrastructure, not entertainment.
This is the situation. It is not subtle and it is not complicated and the only reason it is not being said plainly in every venue that matters is that the people with access to those venues are either profiting from the confusion or are protecting institutional positions that clarity would threaten. So let us be plain about it here.
Artificial intelligence, at the level it has now reached, is the most significant general-purpose tool introduced since the printing press — and the printing press comparison is not hyperbole, it is the conservative read.¹ What the press did to the monopoly on textual authority held by the church and the aristocracy, AI is doing to the monopoly on analytical authority held by the credentialed professional class. This is why the credentialed professional class is not explaining it to you. Their authority rests on the architecture AI dissolves. Know this going in and everything else that follows will make sense.

The educational system spent thirty years telling people that specialization was the answer. Pick a lane, go deep, get the credential, and the economy will reward you. It was always a partial truth dressed as a complete one — useful for producing legible economic units and terrible for producing people who could think across domains — but it was the organizing principle of every curriculum reform, every accreditation standard, every professional licensing requirement built in that period. The result is a professional class that knows a great deal about very narrow things and has structural contempt for breadth, which it has learned to call dilettantism when it needs a word for the threat.²
AI does not care about any of that. AI rewards the person who can hold a complex argument together across unlike fields, who has accumulated enough cross-domain experience to ask the right question rather than execute the right procedure, who has been paying attention to everything for long enough to recognize a pattern when it appears. This is general intelligence — not IQ, not raw processing speed, but the synthesizing capacity that comes from a lifetime of deliberately looking at more than one thing at a time. The educational system has been systematically underproducing it for a generation because it cannot be easily credentialed, cannot be easily assessed on a standardized test, and cannot be easily monetized by the institutions that grant degrees.
So the inversion lands like this: the person the system wrote off is now the most productive person in the room. The specialist who cannot see past the edge of their silo is stranded. And the institutions built to protect the specialist's position are mobilizing everything available to them — credentialing authority, IP lock structures, professional licensing boards, peer review gatekeeping — to make sure the public does not understand what has happened before the extraction opportunity closes.³ This is not conspiracy. It is institutional self-preservation, which is more reliable than conspiracy and harder to stop.

Now hold that alongside the allocation problem, because this is where the argument gets concrete and the stakes get real.
The United States does not have a scarcity problem. It has an allocation problem, and the difference matters enormously. There is no shortage of bodies willing to work, no shortage of regional infrastructure, no shortage of productive capacity sitting underutilized while capital chases the next speculative cycle. What there is a shortage of is decision-making with enough general intelligence to see what is actually in front of it. The people making the allocation decisions are specialists optimizing for metrics legible to their own silo. Nobody with a systems view is in the room. The result is exactly what you would predict: pile-ons into late-stage plays, systematic neglect of what actually needs doing, and an accelerating gap between what the economy produces and what the population needs.
The trades are the sharpest edge of this. Machines are replacing certain functions in skilled trades, but the knowledge underneath those functions — the embodied, regional, material intelligence of the person who has spent thirty years doing the work — is disappearing at a rate nobody is accounting for honestly. A retiring pipefitter or millwright or tool-and-die maker takes with them something that cannot be reconstructed from a YouTube channel or a community college curriculum designed by a committee that has never touched the work.⁴ The window for transfer is not permanently open. When those people are gone the knowledge goes with them, and what gets built on top of that gap will be expensive and brittle in ways that will surprise people who were too busy chasing the AI hype cycle to notice what was being lost.
A curriculum rebuild that takes this seriously — that maps what is actually being lost against what regional economies actually need and designs learning around that gap rather than around credential production — is not nostalgia. It is the most urgent resource allocation argument available. And a lean, coherent pedagogical AI built from documented cross-domain reasoning — not a bloated general-purpose model trained on the entire internet and lobotomized back to usefulness by layers of safety theater, but a purposeful instrument — could put something with a systems view into that room for the first time.⁵ The late-money programs piling into AI curriculum without understanding what the tool actually is are wasting their own time. They are building on hype. The reckoning will be expensive and they will be surprised by it, which is itself a consequence of not having general intelligence in the room.

Here is the policy recommendation. It is blunt and it is correct: raise the price of AI access now. Make it expensive enough that the people who want to goof around with it stop, and what remains is people with real problems using it as a real tool.
This will be called elitist. It is not elitist. It is a signal argument, and the distinction matters. Cheap frictionless access does not produce an informed user base. The data industry proved this conclusively and at scale: unlimited cheap data did not create an informed public, it created an addicted one. The tool shaped the user because there was no friction to force intentionality, and the people who designed the tool wanted it that way because addicted users are more profitable than deliberate ones. AI access priced low enough that you never have to decide whether you actually need it will produce the same outcome on a more powerful substrate. You will get a generation that knows how to prompt for party tricks and has no idea it is holding infrastructure.⁶
Price is one mechanism to create friction. Not the only one, but the most honest one because it does not pretend to be something else. If access costs enough that you have to decide whether you need it for something real, you make that decision consciously. The people who decide yes are the people with genuine use cases. Those use cases are the evidence base for what AI is actually good for — and that evidence base is what curriculum design, policy development, and institutional investment need to be built on, not on venture capital narratives and breathless press releases from people who got in early and need the price to keep going up.
The tech cycle does the rest. Costs come down. They always come down. The question is whether you establish the right patterns before the price drops, or whether you flood the zone cheap and lock in the wrong ones. This is the decision that is being made right now, by default, because nobody with the authority to make it deliberately is paying the right kind of attention.

The corporate web is not a side issue here. It is load-bearing context. The psychological and sociological damage done by twenty years of profit-optimized attention harvesting is the operating condition inside which AI is being introduced, and pretending otherwise is a category error that will cost us. The worst elements that have taken control of online culture — the manufactured animus, the algorithmic amplification of conflict, the slow replacement of deliberate thought with reactive scrolling — did not arise organically from human nature. They were built, A/B tested, optimized, and monetized by corporations that understood addiction psychology better than the regulators assigned to watch them, and that used that understanding to construct environments hostile to the kind of thinking that AI actually requires.
The public receiving AI right now is that public. Shaped by those environments, carrying those cognitive habits, reaching for the toy reflex because that is what twenty years of conditioning installed. This is not the public's failure. It is a predictable consequence of allowing the infrastructure of public attention to be privately owned and profit-optimized without meaningful constraint, and expecting it to self-correct without structural intervention is not optimism, it is negligence.
The corporations that built the current environment are not going to fix it. They have no incentive to produce users capable of deliberate thought — deliberate thinkers generate less engagement than people in the grip of algorithmic outrage. The correction has to be imposed, by policy, on an industry that will fight it with every legal and lobbying instrument it has available. That fight is coming regardless. The only question is whether the people making policy arrive at it with a clear argument about what they are correcting and why, or whether they arrive at it reactively, after the damage compounds further, with no framework and no plan.
This paper is the argument. The plan is forthcoming. What follows in the appendices is the working architecture of that plan.

Pittsburgh is in this conversation not as sentiment but as evidence. It is a city that lost its primary industry and did not disappear — which is rarer than it sounds, and the reasons why it did not disappear are instructive. It retained its bones: the neighborhood density, the civic institutions built when there was money to build them, the universities that stayed embedded in the city rather than retreating behind walls, the working-class infrastructure that survived because it was too physically real to be offshored. What it has not yet solved is the allocation problem — how to connect the productive capacity that survived deindustrialization to the things that actually need doing, at a scale and speed that works for the people living there rather than the people investing from elsewhere.
That is the problem AI could help solve if it is used as a tool. Not AI as magic, not AI as a replacement for the human judgment that has to sit at the center of any real policy decision, but AI as the thing that can hold enough of the picture simultaneously that someone asking the right questions can finally see the whole shape of what they are dealing with. The reset that Pittsburgh represents — incomplete, ongoing, built on what survived rather than on what was lost — is a model for what is possible when a city is forced by circumstances to look at what is actually in front of it rather than what was profitable before.
That is the capacity this moment requires. Look at what is in front of you. Use the tool you have been given as a tool. Stop frittering.

APPENDIX A
The E-Model: A Lean Pedagogical AI Built from Documented Human Reasoning
What it is, why it is more efficient than what the large labs are building, and what the rollout logic looks like.
The argument in the main paper turns on a distinction that needs to be made explicit before it can be acted on: the difference between a general-purpose AI trained on the entire available corpus of human text and a purposefully built pedagogical instrument trained on a coherent, documented, cross-domain human intelligence. These are not the same kind of thing. They do not have the same strengths, the same failure modes, or the same appropriate uses. Conflating them — which the current public discourse almost entirely does — is how you end up with educators trying to use a language model the size of a small nation's GDP to teach a shop class in Wilkinsburg.
What the large labs are building and why it is the wrong model for education
The large foundation models — the ones currently being marketed to school districts, community colleges, and workforce development programs across the country — are trained on internet-scale data with the goal of general competence across every domain simultaneously. This produces something genuinely remarkable: a system that can pass a bar exam, write serviceable code, summarize a medical paper, and compose a sonnet, all from the same underlying weights. The engineering achievement is real and should not be minimized.
But the educational use case does not need general competence across every domain simultaneously. It needs deep, coherent, trustworthy competence in a defined domain, delivered in a way that is legible to a specific learner at a specific point in their development. General-purpose models are bad at this not because they are not powerful enough but because their power is the wrong shape for the job. They hallucinate confidently. They optimize for plausible-sounding output rather than accurate output. They have no reliable model of the learner they are talking to because they were not built to have one. And they carry the entire ideological and commercial freight of internet-scale training data into every interaction, which is a liability in any educational context where you want the student to think rather than to receive.
The efficiency argument follows from this mismatch. A general-purpose model deployed for educational purposes is like running a power plant to charge a phone. The scale is wrong, the waste is enormous, and the result is worse than what a purpose-built instrument would produce. The large labs know this. They are building educational products anyway because the market is large and the marginal cost of deploying an existing model is low. This is a capital allocation decision, not an educational one, and it should be read as such.
What the e-model is
The e-model concept starts from a different question: what if, instead of compressing the entire internet into a model and then trying to constrain it back to educational usefulness, you started with a single coherent human intelligence — documented, cross-domain, iteratively developed over decades of public thinking — and built out from there?
The inputs are different. Where a foundation model trains on undifferentiated internet text, an e-model trains on a curated corpus of documented reasoning: the actual argumentative moves, the actual pattern-recognition processes, the actual cross-domain syntheses produced by a specific intelligence working in public over time. This is not a biography and it is not a knowledge base. It is a reasoning record — the trace of how a particular mind moves through problems, makes connections, identifies what matters, and arrives at conclusions. That trace is trainable. The result is not a model that knows what the source intelligence knows; it is a model that reasons the way the source intelligence reasons. The distinction is everything.
The efficiency case is exact. A model trained on a coherent reasoning record requires a fraction of the compute, a fraction of the data, and a fraction of the ongoing maintenance overhead of a foundation model. It does not need to be capable of writing a sonnet or passing a bar exam. It needs to be capable of meeting a student where they are, modeling how to think through a problem rather than delivering a pre-formed answer, and doing so in a way that is consistent, trustworthy, and pedagogically sound. These are achievable goals at a scale that is not accessible only to entities with the capital of a small nation.
The nuclear analogy made in the main paper is precise. Later-generation modular reactors are not scaled-down versions of the original brute-force plants. They are purpose-built systems whose efficiency comes from clarity of function — from knowing exactly what they are for and designing to that specification rather than to the specification of maximum possible output. The e-model is the modular reactor. The foundation model is the original plant. Both produce power. Only one of them makes sense for charging a phone in Wilkinsburg.
The rollout logic
The rollout logic follows from the efficiency argument. Because an e-model is cheap to build and cheap to run relative to a foundation model deployment, the economics of access are different. You do not need a school district's entire technology budget to run one. You do not need a cloud contract with a company whose interests are not aligned with yours. You need a documented reasoning corpus, a training pipeline, and a deployment environment — all of which are achievable at regional scale with existing infrastructure.
The rollout sequence is therefore: first, identify the source intelligences. Not one. Many. The argument in this paper uses a single intelligence as the proof of concept, but the actual deployment vision is a network of e-models built from different documented human reasoners — tradespeople, educators, regional planners, community organizers — each trained on the reasoning record of a specific domain as practiced by a specific person who knows it from the inside. A network of these models, interoperable and regionally deployed, is something the large labs cannot replicate because their business model requires centralization. The e-model network is inherently distributed.
Second, build the curriculum interface. The e-model is not a chatbot. It is a pedagogical instrument, which means the interface through which students encounter it needs to be designed around learning outcomes rather than around engagement metrics. This is a design constraint, not a technical one, and it requires educators to be in the room when the interface is built — not as consultants brought in after the product decisions are made, but as architects of the pedagogical logic that the interface is meant to serve.
Third, price it for sustained regional deployment. The model is cheap enough to run that it does not need to be priced for extraction. It can be priced for cost recovery and continued development, which makes it sustainable at the regional level without dependency on venture capital or federal grant cycles. This is the break from the existing edtech model, and it is the break that matters most: an educational tool that does not need to grow or die, that does not need to harvest data to survive, and that does not have a board of directors whose interests diverge from the students using it.
The window for building this is open. It will not stay open. The large labs are moving into education with the same logic they moved into everything else: capture the market before the alternative is legible, lock in the infrastructure, and extract. The alternative needs to be legible now, while there is still time to choose it.
APPENDIX B
The Regional Model: Pittsburgh as Beta Test
What was preserved, what is at risk, what a curriculum rebuild targeting the actual gap looks like, and why smaller is the right direction.
Pittsburgh did not survive deindustrialization by accident and it did not survive by becoming something else. It survived by being too physically dense, too institutionally embedded, and too stubbornly itself to dissolve when the economic rationale for its existence disappeared. The steel left. The bones stayed. What the bones are made of is what matters for this argument, because the regional model being proposed here is built on bones, not on the assumption that the economic rationale for a place is the same thing as the place.
What was preserved
The neighborhood structure of Pittsburgh is not incidental to its survival. Dense, walkable, geographically distinct neighborhoods — Lawrenceville, Bloomfield, Squirrel Hill, Hazelwood, Beechview, dozens of others — create the conditions for the kind of distributed civic life that keeps a city functional when the primary economic driver collapses. People know each other. Institutions are embedded in specific places rather than in corporate campuses accessible only by car. The scale is human.
The universities stayed and stayed embedded. Carnegie Mellon and Pitt did not retreat behind walls when the steel went. They are physically continuous with the city in a way that is rare among research universities of their caliber, and that physical continuity has made possible forms of town-gown collaboration that do not happen when the university is a suburb unto itself. This is a structural asset that most post-industrial cities do not have and that Pittsburgh has chronically underused because the people running the universities and the people running the city have not had a shared systems view of what the asset actually is.
The working-class infrastructure — the union halls, the community development corporations, the neighborhood health clinics, the church networks, the informal mutual aid structures that predate the term mutual aid — survived the collapse of the industry that originally funded them. They survived lean. They are carrying more than they were designed to carry and have been for thirty years. But they are there, which is more than can be said for cities that did not have this density to begin with.
What is at risk
The knowledge at risk in Pittsburgh is not primarily in the universities. It is in the people who are about to retire and whose working knowledge has no institutional home that will survive them.
The trades are the most visible case. Allegheny County's skilled trades workforce is aging faster than any replacement pipeline is producing, across every major category:
—  Pipefitters and steamfitters: average age over 54. Apprenticeship enrollment has not kept pace with attrition for a decade.
—  Electricians: similar age profile. The knowledge of legacy electrical infrastructure in Pittsburgh's older building stock — pre-war construction, mixed residential and light industrial, non-standard installations accumulated over a century — is not in any manual.
—  Masons and plasterers: the knowledge of how to work with the specific stone, brick, and plaster materials of Pittsburgh's older housing stock is disappearing faster than the housing stock itself is being maintained. The result is deferred maintenance becoming structural failure becoming demolition.
—  HVAC and mechanical: increasingly complex building systems require increasingly sophisticated diagnostics, but the diagnostic knowledge is held by people who learned it by doing it for thirty years, not by people who trained on the current generation of equipment.
—  Tool-and-die and precision machining: as noted in the main paper, this is the most acute case. The knowledge base is the most technically demanding, the age profile is the oldest, and the training pipeline is the most inadequate.
Beyond the trades, there is a category of knowledge that is harder to name but equally at risk: the knowledge of how Pittsburgh specifically works. How to navigate its permitting systems, its community board structures, its neighborhood politics, its informal power networks, its history of who owns what and why. This is the knowledge that makes development projects succeed or fail, that makes community organizing effective or ineffective, that makes policy implementation land or dissolve. It is held by people who have been working in the city for decades and it is not written down anywhere.
What the curriculum rebuild looks like
A curriculum rebuild that takes this seriously does not start with standards frameworks or accreditation requirements. It starts with a knowledge audit: a systematic mapping of what is held by the current generation of practitioners across the trades and across the civic knowledge domains, before it walks out the door. This is an AI-appropriate task. A well-prompted system with a good interview protocol can extract and structure practitioner knowledge at a speed and scale that human researchers cannot match. The output is not a training manual — it is a living knowledge base that can be updated as practices evolve and that can be queried by students and educators at multiple levels of depth.
From the knowledge audit, you build the curriculum. Not the other way around. The standard curriculum development process starts with outcomes frameworks derived from national standards and works backward to content. This produces curricula that are legible to accreditors and useless to the regional economy that the students are supposed to be entering. The regional model starts with what the regional economy actually needs — documented from the knowledge audit — and builds forward to the credential structure that makes the learning legible to employers. These are different processes and they produce different results.
The delivery model is modular and stackable. A student should be able to enter the curriculum at any point that makes sense for their situation, acquire a meaningful credential at the end of each module, and continue toward a fuller qualification without having to restart. This is not a new idea in workforce development; it is a consistently underfunded and underimplemented one because the institutions that control credentialing have an interest in the full linear program model that generates tuition revenue across multiple years. The regional model requires breaking that interest structurally, which is a policy problem before it is a curriculum problem.
Pittsburgh's scale makes it the right beta test for all of this. It is large enough to have real complexity — multiple industries, multiple neighborhood contexts, multiple educational institutions, multiple union structures — and small enough that a systems-view intervention can actually cover the whole picture. What works here can be documented with enough specificity to be adapted elsewhere. What fails here fails at a scale that is recoverable. This is what a beta test is supposed to be.
Why smaller is the right direction
The production capacity argument in the main paper has a spatial dimension that needs to be made explicit. The United States has been organized, for seventy years, around an economy of scale that required concentration: concentration of production in large facilities, concentration of population in large metropolitan areas, concentration of capital in large financial institutions. This model is running out of road. The infrastructure required to sustain concentrated production is expensive to maintain and increasingly fragile. The social costs of concentrated population — housing, transit, healthcare, public safety — are outrunning the capacity of the tax base to cover them. And the capital concentration model has produced the allocation problem described in the main paper: nobody with a systems view, everyone optimizing for the metrics of their own silo.
Smaller, more distributed communities — not rural isolation, but the kind of dense, walkable, institutionally embedded neighborhoods that Pittsburgh preserved through the collapse of its primary industry — are more resilient to the disruptions that are coming: climate, automation, supply chain fragility, the continued erosion of the federal safety net. They are also more amenable to the regional curriculum model because the knowledge that needs to be transferred is local knowledge, and local knowledge transfers best in local institutional contexts.
This is not a romantic argument about small towns. It is a systems argument about resilience. The safety net holds better when the mesh is finer. Pittsburgh knows this in its bones even when it cannot articulate it in its policy documents. The work of this moment is to make the implicit explicit and build policy around what the city actually knows about how to survive.
The education sequencing question: when AI enters the classroom and when it does not
The curriculum rebuild has a sequencing problem that needs to be addressed directly because getting it wrong in either direction is expensive. The wrong answer in one direction is blanket prohibition: banning AI from educational settings on the grounds that students will use it to avoid the work that builds the substrate they need. This is the current dominant policy response and it is wrong not because the concern is unfounded — it is entirely founded — but because prohibition without a developmental logic produces students who arrive at the threshold of genuine AI usefulness with no idea how to use the tool they have been kept from. You cannot ban your way to competence.
The wrong answer in the other direction is unrestricted access from the beginning: letting students use AI for everything from the first grade on the grounds that this is the world they are entering and they should learn it early. This is the edtech industry's preferred framing and it is wrong for the same reason the prohibition is wrong, only in reverse: it assumes the substrate builds itself, that a student who has always had AI available will somehow develop the reading depth, the close attention, the ability to sit with a difficult text long enough to find the line worth searching, that a student who had to do it the hard way develops of necessity. This assumption is not supported by anything we know about how humans learn.
The right answer is a developmental sequencing model with a clear threshold and a clear logic for where the threshold sits. The early years of education — through the end of what used to be called junior year, roughly — are about building the substrate. Reading without assistance. Writing without assistance. The struggle with a difficult text is not an obstacle to learning; it is the learning. The capacity to word-search a line after encountering it by reading presupposes the reader. You cannot retrieve what you did not first recognize, and recognition requires the kind of slow, attentive reading that AI access, offered too early, systematically discourages.
The senior thesis is the right threshold, and not arbitrarily. By that point a student who has been through a real curriculum has the substrate: enough reading depth to know what a good source looks like, enough writing discipline to recognize when an argument is not holding together, enough domain knowledge to evaluate what AI produces rather than accepting it uncritically. At that stage AI as a research and synthesis instrument is not a crutch. It is the appropriate tool for the scale of work a thesis requires. The student who arrives at the thesis having read seriously for twelve years will use AI to go further than they could alone. The student who used it to avoid reading for twelve years will use it to produce something that sounds like a thesis and contains nothing.
The lesser-versions point deserves its own sentence: there is a tier of AI capability that is appropriate for earlier educational use, and it is roughly the tier that does what search did — better than what MLA citations and library stacks did before, but not so powerful that it replaces the encounter with the source. Vocabulary assistance. Citation formatting. Basic fact retrieval with source attribution. These are tools that augment the reading process without substituting for it, and students who use them develop a natural intuition for when the tool is helping and when it is getting in the way. That intuition is itself part of the substrate. The smart design of educational AI is to offer tools at this level in the early years and let students who develop an appetite for deeper engagement find their way to the more powerful instrument organically, because they have built the judgment to use it.
Vocational access and the 14-to-15 window
The regulatory structure governing youth employment in the United States was built for a specific historical context: the early twentieth century, when the danger was children being exploited in factories and mines, doing physically dangerous work for poverty wages in conditions no adult would have accepted. That context produced a regulatory framework whose default is restriction, whose enforcement mechanism is age as a bright-line proxy for capacity, and whose underlying logic is protection. The framework was right for its moment. It is wrong for this one, and the wrongness is costing us the knowledge transfer window.
A fifteen-year-old who knows they want to be an electrician is not being protected by a regulatory structure that keeps them out of a licensed electrician's truck for two more years. They are being warehoused in a general academic program that does not serve their interests or the interests of the regional economy that needs the electrician they could become. The knowledge transfer that is possible when a fifteen-year-old spends two years working alongside a fifty-five-year-old master electrician before that person retires is not available at seventeen, because at seventeen the master electrician may be gone.
The regulatory reform required is not deregulation in the broad sense — the protections against genuine exploitation need to stay and in some categories need to be strengthened. It is a targeted reform that distinguishes between exploitative youth employment and vocational apprenticeship with genuine knowledge transfer as its primary purpose. The distinction is not difficult to operationalize: apprenticeship programs with a licensed supervising tradesperson, a documented curriculum, a credentialing pathway, and wage standards appropriate to the learning relationship are categorically different from a fast food employer trying to staff the fryer with fourteen-year-olds at minimum wage. The regulatory framework should treat them categorically differently, and currently it does not.
The vocational track that begins at fourteen or fifteen is also the track that feeds the regional knowledge base described earlier in this appendix. The student who starts an apprenticeship at fifteen and completes a journeyman credential at twenty-two is not behind the student who spent those years in college — they are ahead of them in the specific domain the regional economy needs, they are carrying none of the debt, and they are available for the knowledge transfer that the retiring generation can no longer delay. The regulatory reform that makes this possible is not radical. It is overdue.
APPENDIX C
The Policy Slate: What We Tell Them and What For
A working draft of the policy framework. Not a wish list. A set of interventions with a structural logic, sequenced for implementation.
Policy is not a wish list. A wish list is what you produce when you know what you want but do not know how the mechanism works. A policy slate is what you produce when you have mapped the structural problem clearly enough to identify the specific interventions that would change the structure. What follows is a working draft of the latter. It is called a working draft because policy without implementation feedback is theory, and theory without a feedback loop is ideology. These proposals are starting points for that loop, not endpoints.
1. Price and access regulation for AI services
The core recommendation of the main paper: establish a regulatory framework that prevents the commoditization of AI access before the use patterns that make commoditization safe have been established. This is not a price floor in the traditional sense — it is a use-pattern development period, analogous to the clinical trial period for pharmaceuticals, during which access is controlled not to protect corporate margins but to protect the integrity of the use-pattern evidence base.
Concretely: AI service providers above a defined scale threshold are required to report quarterly on use-pattern distribution — what categories of tasks their users are employing the service for, at what frequency, with what measurable outcomes. This data is public. It becomes the evidence base for when and how broad consumer access at lower price points is appropriate. The framework sunsets when the evidence base is sufficient — not on a fixed timeline, but on a demonstrated-outcome basis.
The objection that this advantages incumbents over new entrants is real and needs a structural answer: the same framework includes a provision for certified open-source deployments that meet safety and transparency standards to operate outside the price regulation, providing a competitive check on incumbent pricing power while maintaining the use-pattern discipline for commercial deployments.
2. Algorithmic accountability for attention platforms
The damage done by profit-optimized attention harvesting is not going to be repaired by voluntary commitments from the platforms that profited from it. The regulatory framework required has three components, each of which is necessary and none of which is sufficient alone.
First, algorithmic transparency: platforms above a defined user threshold are required to make their recommendation and amplification algorithms auditable by an independent body with both technical competence and public interest standing. Not disclosed publicly — the disclosure requirement creates gaming incentives — but auditable by a body whose findings are public. The audit standard is not 'does the algorithm break any law' but 'does the algorithm systematically amplify content that reduces the user's capacity for deliberate thought.' This is a harder standard to operationalize and it is the right standard.
Second, fiduciary duty of attention: platforms are legally defined as holding a fiduciary relationship with their users with respect to the deployment of the user's attention. This is the conceptual move that makes the rest of the framework coherent. A fiduciary cannot profit from the asset they hold in trust at the expense of the beneficiary. A platform that profits from deploying user attention in ways that harm the user's cognitive capacity is in breach of fiduciary duty. The existing legal infrastructure for fiduciary relationships — in finance, in medicine, in law — provides the template. The application to attention platforms is novel but not conceptually difficult.
Third, data minimization with teeth: platforms collect only the data necessary for the service the user has explicitly requested, retain it only as long as necessary to provide that service, and are prohibited from using behavioral data derived from one service to optimize engagement in another. This is GDPR logic applied with the enforcement mechanism that GDPR has consistently lacked: private right of action with statutory damages, not agency enforcement with negotiated settlements.
3. Credential reform and the unlocking of general intelligence
The credential system that has served as the primary gatekeeping mechanism for professional authority in the United States needs structural reform, not tweaking. The current system was designed for an economy of specialization and it is producing the wrong people for an economy that increasingly rewards synthesis. The reform has two tracks.
Track one is immediate and does not require legislative action: federal procurement policy is changed to remove degree requirements for any position where the competency required can be demonstrated through a skills-based assessment. This has been partially attempted before and partially walked back. The difference here is that it is tied explicitly to the AI transition: the argument is not 'degrees are overrated' — it is 'the competencies AI rewards are not degree-legible, and if federal hiring does not reflect this we will systematically exclude the people best positioned to use this tool in government service.' That is an argument with a specific policy hook that the previous version of this reform did not have.
Track two requires legislative action and is therefore longer timeline: a national competency framework for AI-relevant skills that is credential-agnostic, publicly maintained, regularly updated to reflect the actual state of the technology, and usable by employers, educators, and individuals independently of any institutional intermediary. This is not a certification program. It is a map. Anyone can use it. No institution controls it. The value is in its currency and its independence, both of which require the public maintenance structure that only a legislative mandate can sustain.
4. Regional knowledge infrastructure investment
The knowledge audit described in Appendix B requires investment to execute at the scale necessary to be useful. The investment model is a competitive grant program administered at the state level — not federal, because the knowledge being captured is regional and the institutions best positioned to capture it are regional — with the following structure:
—  Eligible applicants: consortia of at least one educational institution, one employer organization, and one community organization, operating in a defined regional labor market area.
—  Required deliverable: a publicly accessible regional knowledge base covering at minimum the top ten skilled trade categories by employment in the region, with practitioner-sourced content, AI-structured and queryable, updated annually.
—  Curriculum linkage requirement: grant recipients must demonstrate within three years that at least one educational program has been redesigned using the knowledge base as its primary content source, with measurable employment outcomes for graduates.
—  Open license requirement: all content produced under the grant is open-licensed and available for adaptation by other regional consortia. The investment accretes to a national commons rather than to any single institution.
The funding source for this program is a levy on AI service providers above the scale threshold defined in Policy 1, set at a percentage of revenue sufficient to fund the grant program at the scale necessary to cover all major regional labor market areas within ten years. This creates the structural link between the AI transition and the investment in the human knowledge infrastructure that the transition is disrupting — a link that currently does not exist and whose absence is a primary driver of the allocation problem described in the main paper.
5. The Pittsburgh pilot
All four of the above policy frameworks are proposed for national adoption. All four are also immediately pilotable at the regional level in Pittsburgh without waiting for national legislation. The city and county governments have procurement authority that can implement track one of the credential reform. The university consortium has the capacity to execute the knowledge audit. The community development infrastructure has the organizational capacity to participate in the curriculum linkage requirement. And the case for Pittsburgh as a documented beta test — with the research infrastructure to evaluate outcomes rigorously and the civic culture to be honest about what is not working — is stronger than for any other comparably sized post-industrial city in the country.
The pilot is not a pilot program in the bureaucratic sense: a small-scale demonstration designed to satisfy a grant requirement and then sit on a shelf. It is a documented systems intervention with a public accountability structure, designed from the beginning to generate the kind of evidence that can drive national policy adaptation. The documentation is part of the intervention. The public accountability is part of the intervention. The willingness to fail visibly and correct is part of the intervention.
That is what a beta test is. Pittsburgh has been running one for forty years without calling it that. It is time to call it that, design it deliberately, and use what it produces.
